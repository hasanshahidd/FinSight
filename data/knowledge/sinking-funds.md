# Sinking Funds — Stop Being Surprised by "Surprise" Expenses

A **sinking fund** is a savings target you contribute to monthly so that when an irregular expense arrives, the money is already there. The name comes from accounting: a "sinking fund" sets aside money to retire a future debt.

The premise: most "emergencies" aren't emergencies — they're predictable expenses you didn't plan for. Property taxes, car registration, holiday gifts, annual insurance, replacing a laptop. These don't surprise you in *whether* they'll happen — they surprise you because the amount lands in one chunk.

## The sinking-fund move

Instead of being broadsided in November by a $1,200 holiday-gift bill:

> $1,200 ÷ 12 months = $100/month into a "Holidays" sinking fund.

Now December feels like nothing. The pain was distributed across the year.

## Common sinking funds

| Category | Annual estimate | Monthly contribution |
|---|---|---|
| Holidays / gifts | $600–$1,500 | $50–$125 |
| Annual auto insurance | $800–$2,000 | $65–$170 |
| Property tax (if not escrowed) | $1,500–$8,000 | $125–$670 |
| Car maintenance + repairs | $600–$1,500 | $50–$125 |
| Vehicle replacement | $300–$600/month if planning a $20–30k car in 5 years | |
| Vacation | varies | $100–$400 |
| Annual subscriptions | varies | sum / 12 |
| Home maintenance | 1–2% of home value/yr | $100–$400 |
| Pet health | $300–$1,200 | $25–$100 |
| Medical out-of-pocket (after HSA) | varies | $50–$200 |

## How to set them up

Two practical patterns:

1. **One savings account, multiple "buckets" tracked in a spreadsheet.** Simpler. Most people pick this.
2. **Multiple labeled sub-accounts** (Ally, SoFi, Capital One 360 all support this). Cleaner mental model — when the auto-insurance bill arrives, you transfer from "Auto Insurance" to checking.

Automate the monthly transfer the day after payday. Treat sinking funds as bills.

## Sinking funds vs. emergency fund

- **Sinking funds**: known expenses, known amounts, predictable timing.
- **Emergency fund**: unknown expenses (job loss, urgent car repair, ER visit not covered by HSA buffer), 3–6 months of *core* expenses.

These are separate. Don't dip into the emergency fund for predictable holiday gifts; you'll exhaust it on non-emergencies.

## Common mistakes

- **Underfunding** by guessing low. Track what you actually spent on each category last year and base contributions on that.
- **Not adjusting**. A sinking fund at $50/month that's $400 short every December needs to be $90/month.
- **Folding sinking funds into one mental "savings"** number. Makes it impossible to know if you have funded the holidays + the auto insurance + the vacation, or just one of them.
- **Skipping a month "just this once."** This is the gateway to the original problem. Skip = future surprise.

## When you can stop

For purely predictable annual expenses, the sinking fund is steady-state. For irregular ones (vehicle replacement, home maintenance), the fund grows over years and may be partially drawn down only periodically. The goal isn't to hit a particular balance; it's to make every expense feel routine.
