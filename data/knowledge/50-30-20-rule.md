# The 50/30/20 Budgeting Rule

The 50/30/20 rule is a simple budgeting framework popularized by Senator Elizabeth Warren in *All Your Worth*. It splits your **after-tax income** into three buckets:

- **50% Needs** — rent or mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance, transportation, minimum debt payments. These are expenses you cannot easily skip.
- **30% Wants** — dining out, streaming subscriptions, hobbies, travel, non-essential shopping. Lifestyle spending you choose.
- **20% Savings & Debt Repayment** — emergency fund contributions, retirement (401k/IRA), investment accounts, and any debt payments above the minimum.

## How to apply it

1. Calculate monthly take-home pay (after taxes and pre-tax retirement contributions).
2. Tally each category for the past 60–90 days using your transactions.
3. Compare actual spend to the 50/30/20 split. If Needs > 50%, look at fixed-cost reductions (housing, insurance shop-around). If Wants > 30%, identify the top 3 lifestyle categories and trim. If Savings < 20%, automate a transfer on payday.

## When the rule breaks

In high-cost-of-living cities, Needs may legitimately exceed 50%. A common adaptation is **60/20/20** or **70/20/10** while you grow income. The discipline is the *rank order* — pay yourself first via automated savings, then needs, then wants — not the exact percentages.

## Compared to other frameworks

- **Zero-based budget**: every dollar gets a job; more granular but requires more effort.
- **Pay-yourself-first**: a savings target comes off the top, the rest is unconstrained.
- **Envelope system**: hard caps per category, paid in cash or via separate accounts.

The 50/30/20 rule's strength is simplicity: three buckets to monitor, no spreadsheet required.
