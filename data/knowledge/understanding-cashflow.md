# Understanding Personal Cash Flow

**Cash flow** is the difference between money coming in and money going out over a defined period. It's the most important metric in personal finance — net worth grows when cash flow is positive over time.

## The basic equation

> Cash flow = Income − Spending

Positive cash flow means you saved or invested. Negative means you took on debt or drew down assets. Tracking it over a rolling 30 or 90 day window smooths out one-time spikes.

## Income — be conservative

Use **net (after-tax) income** for cash-flow purposes. Pre-tax income overstates what you can spend. Variable income (commission, freelance, bonuses) should be tracked at the **trailing 6-month average**, not the best month.

## Spending — categorize at three levels

- **Fixed costs** — same every month. Rent, mortgage, insurance, subscriptions, debt minimums. Hard to flex without major life changes.
- **Variable necessities** — groceries, transportation, utilities, household. Can flex 20–30% with effort.
- **Discretionary** — dining out, entertainment, shopping, hobbies. Highest flex; first to cut when needed.

A healthy ratio is roughly **fixed ≤ 50%, variable ≈ 30%, discretionary + savings ≈ 20%**. When fixed costs creep above 60%, financial flexibility collapses.

## The cash-flow waterfall

Money lands somewhere. The order matters:

1. **Taxes** — taken before you see it.
2. **Pre-tax retirement** — 401(k), HSA. Reduces taxable income.
3. **Fixed costs** — typically autopaid early in the month.
4. **Savings transfers** — automate these to fire the day after payday so they happen *before* discretionary spending.
5. **Variable spending** — what's left funds groceries, gas, entertainment.

The classic mistake is putting savings *last* in the waterfall. Whatever's "left" is reliably zero.

## Cash flow vs. net worth

Cash flow is the *engine*; net worth is the *mileage*. Strong cash flow with no investing builds a big checking account but stagnates net worth (inflation erodes it). Poor cash flow with great investing reverses gains via lifestyle creep. You need both: positive cash flow, deployed into appreciating assets.

## How to improve cash flow

In rough order of impact:

1. **Income side** — raise, side income, switch jobs. Often the largest lever.
2. **Fixed-cost reduction** — refinance debt, shop insurance, downsize housing.
3. **Variable-cost discipline** — top three categories typically contain most of the opportunity.
4. **Tax efficiency** — capture all pre-tax/Roth space, HSA, dependent care FSA.

Making more is more powerful than spending less, but spending discipline is what *keeps* the gains.
