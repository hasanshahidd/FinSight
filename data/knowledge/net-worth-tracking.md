# Net Worth Tracking - The Single Most Important Number

Net worth is **assets minus liabilities**. It's the most honest measure of financial progress because it cannot be gamed by credit cards, refinancing, or one-time windfalls. Every other metric (income, savings rate, "checking balance") is a leading or lagging indicator of net worth.

## How to calculate

Sum **assets**:
- Cash & checking accounts
- Savings, CDs, money market, HYSA
- Brokerage / taxable investments (current value)
- 401(k), IRA, HSA, 403(b), 457 (current value)
- Real estate (current market value, not purchase price)
- Cars (current resale value, not purchase price; usually depreciate quickly)
- Crypto (mark to market)

Subtract **liabilities**:
- Credit card balances (statement balance)
- Student loans
- Auto loans
- Mortgage (current principal balance)
- Personal loans, BNPL ("buy now pay later")
- Tax debt (if any)

Net worth = assets − liabilities. It can be negative - common in early career or with student loans.

## What to track and how often

**Monthly is enough.** Daily / weekly invites obsessing over noise; quarterly misses lifestyle creep. A simple spreadsheet works:

| Date | Cash | Savings | Brokerage | 401k | Real estate | Cars | CC debt | Student loans | Mortgage | NW |

Most personal-finance apps (Empower, Monarch, YNAB) automate this with read-only account links.

## Trajectory matters more than the number

Two people with $50k net worth aren't equivalent - one might be growing $1k/month and the other shrinking. Track the **rolling 12-month change**, not just the snapshot.

## Reasonable benchmarks

A common rule of thumb (Stanley & Danko, *The Millionaire Next Door*):
> **Expected net worth = age × pre-tax income / 10**

A 35-year-old earning $80k → expected ~$280k. Falling significantly below this signals lifestyle creep or under-investing; well above suggests you're on the prosperity track.

This is rough - early career, high-cost cities, and student-loan repayment all distort it.

## What net worth is *not*

- **Cash flow** - you can have high net worth and bad cash flow (illiquid in real estate). Both matter.
- **Income** - high earners with high spending often have surprising small net worth.
- **Status** - luxury cars and expensive homes typically *destroy* net worth via depreciation and maintenance.

## Increasing net worth: the only three levers

1. **Earn more.** Raises, side income, switching jobs.
2. **Spend less.** Especially the big three: housing, transportation, food.
3. **Invest the difference at a positive real return.** Index funds, retirement accounts.

Everything else (budgeting apps, cashback hacking, side gigs) is a tactical refinement of one of these three.
