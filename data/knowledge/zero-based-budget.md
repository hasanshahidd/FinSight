# Zero-Based Budgeting

In a zero-based budget, **every dollar of income is assigned a job before the month begins**. Income minus all allocations equals zero. Unlike percentage-based rules, it forces deliberate decisions about each category.

## The setup

1. Estimate monthly income (use the lowest reliable number for variable income).
2. List every fixed expense: rent, utilities, insurance, debt minimums, subscriptions.
3. List variable categories with target amounts: groceries, gas, dining, household.
4. Allocate to savings goals: emergency fund, retirement, sinking funds.
5. Allocate any remaining dollars to discretionary buckets, debt payoff, or investments. Total = income.

## Why it works

Behavioral research shows people overspend in undefined categories. Naming the dollar - "this $200 is for groceries, not for whatever" - reduces leakage. It also surfaces lifestyle creep: when income rises, the gap between income and allocations is visible.

## Common variations

- **YNAB method**: forward-looking, only budget money you already have ("age your money").
- **Sinking funds**: monthly allocations to irregular expenses (annual insurance, car registration, holiday gifts) so they don't break the budget when due.
- **Cash envelope**: zero-based with physical cash for variable categories - modern equivalent is multiple bank accounts.

## When it underperforms

Zero-based budgeting is high-effort. If you don't reconcile transactions weekly, the budget drifts and becomes meaningless. People with very stable expenses or those who already save 20%+ may get most of the benefit from a simpler 50/30/20 rule.

## Tips for sticking with it

- Reconcile weekly, not monthly. Small drift is easy to fix; big drift kills motivation.
- Build a "buffer" or "miscellaneous" line - eliminates the temptation to fudge other categories.
- Review and re-budget every month; do not copy last month's plan blindly.
