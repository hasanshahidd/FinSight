# Insurance Basics — What You Actually Need

Insurance protects against catastrophic losses. The math is simple: pay a small predictable cost (premium) to avoid a rare large unpredictable cost (a loss exceeding what you can cover from savings). The hard part is figuring out which policies you need and which are profit centers for the seller.

## The five policies most adults need

### 1. Health insurance
Non-negotiable in the US. A serious medical event without insurance can be $50k–$500k+. Choose between high-deductible health plans (HDHP, lower premium, eligible for HSA) and PPO/HMO (higher premium, lower out-of-pocket per visit). Optimize for **out-of-pocket maximum** + **provider network**, not just premium.

### 2. Auto insurance
Required by law if you drive. State minimums are usually inadequate — carry at least 100/300/100 (per-person bodily / per-accident bodily / property) and consider an umbrella policy if assets exceed the per-accident cap. Shop quotes annually; rates vary 30–50% between insurers.

### 3. Renters or homeowners insurance
- **Renters**: ~$15–25/month covers your stuff and protects against liability. Your landlord's policy covers the *building*, not your possessions. Worth it.
- **Homeowners**: required by mortgage lenders. Replacement-cost coverage > actual-cash-value. Document possessions with photos for claims.

### 4. Term life insurance
Only needed if someone depends on your income (spouse, kids, aging parents). **Term, not whole life.** A 20–30 year term policy at 30 years old is ~$25/month for $500k coverage. Whole life is 5–10x more expensive and the "investment" component returns ~2% — buy term and invest the difference in index funds.

### 5. Long-term disability insurance
Often the most overlooked. The probability of a 30-year-old becoming disabled before retirement is higher than dying. Many employers offer it for ~$10–30/month. Aim for ~60–70% income replacement.

## Policies most people *do not* need

- **Whole life / Universal life as an investment.** Already mentioned. Bad math.
- **Extended warranties** on consumer electronics. Manufacturers profit ~50% margin on these; the math doesn't work.
- **Identity theft insurance.** Often duplicated by your credit cards; freezing your credit is more effective.
- **Pet insurance**, in most cases. The expected value calculation usually doesn't favor it; self-insure with a savings buffer.
- **Cancer / accident / mortgage insurance** — usually expensive niche policies that overlap with health/disability/life.

## How to set deductibles

Higher deductible = lower premium. The math: pick the deductible at the level you can comfortably absorb from your emergency fund. People often run with a $250 auto deductible because that's the default, but moving to $1,000 typically saves 15–25% of the premium — a faster payback than the difference would take to recoup in claims.

## Annual review

Insurance is one of the few financial line items where shopping the market every 12–24 months reliably saves money. Quotes are free. The same risk profile can vary $300–800/year between insurers.
