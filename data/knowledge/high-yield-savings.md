# High-Yield Savings Accounts (HYSA) - What They Are and Why You Need One

A high-yield savings account is a federally-insured deposit account that pays significantly more interest than a typical big-bank savings account. As of recent years, online HYSAs commonly pay **4–5% APY**, while big-bank savings accounts often pay **0.01–0.05% APY** - a 100x difference on the same money.

## Why most people are losing money to inflation

If your savings sits at 0.01% while inflation runs 3%, your purchasing power drops ~3% per year in real terms. On $10,000 over 5 years that's roughly $1,500 of lost value - for no benefit.

## What to look for

- **APY**, not just "interest rate" (APY accounts for compounding).
- **No monthly fees** and **no minimum balance** for the advertised rate.
- **FDIC-insured** up to $250,000 per depositor per bank (NCUA-insured for credit unions).
- **Easy transfers** to/from your primary checking - usually 1–3 business days via ACH.
- **Reasonable transaction limits** (Reg D was suspended; most banks still cap withdrawals at 6/month from savings).

## Where HYSAs fit

- **Emergency fund** - ideal location. Liquid, safe, growing slightly faster than inflation.
- **Sinking funds** - annual insurance, vacation, holiday gifts.
- **House down-payment** if needed within 1–2 years (longer horizons may justify CDs or short-term bonds).
- **Buffer between paychecks** for variable-income households.

## Where HYSAs do not fit

- Long-term wealth building (10+ years) - equities historically outperform after inflation.
- Day-to-day spending - too much friction; use a checking account.
- Money you might need within hours - ACH transfers take 1–3 days.

## Common providers (US, as of recent years)

Online-only banks tend to offer the highest rates because they have no branches. Brokerage cash management accounts (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard) often pay competitive rates and integrate with investing. Big-name traditional banks rarely offer competitive APYs even for "premium" tiers; switch.

## How to set it up

1. Open an account online (15 minutes; KYC requires SSN, ID, address).
2. Link your checking account via micro-deposits or instant verification.
3. Set up an automated transfer the day after payday.
4. Do not link a debit card to it - the friction is the point.

Promotional rates that drop after 3 months are common; set a calendar reminder to compare again.
