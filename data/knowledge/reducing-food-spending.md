# Reducing Food Spending Without Misery

Food is the most variable line item in most budgets and the easiest place to overspend invisibly. The goal is not to live on rice and beans - it's to capture the gap between *what you actually enjoy* and *what you currently spend*.

## Diagnose first: groceries vs. dining out

Pull 60 days of transactions and split food into:

- **Groceries** (raw ingredients, planned meals)
- **Dining out** (restaurants, takeout, delivery apps, coffee shops, bars)
- **Convenience** (gas-station snacks, work cafeteria, vending)

Most overspending sits in **dining out + convenience**, not groceries. Households often discover they're spending more on food *eaten outside the kitchen* than on groceries.

## High-leverage changes (ranked)

1. **Cap delivery apps.** DoorDash/Uber Eats/Grubhub markups, service fees, and tips run 35–50% over restaurant price. A $25 dinner becomes $40+. Setting a hard limit (e.g., 2 deliveries/month) is among the highest-leverage cuts available.
2. **Plan the week's dinners on Sunday.** Even a rough plan ("Mon-pasta, Tue-tacos, Wed-leftovers") cuts impulse takeout. Decision fatigue at 6pm is the enemy.
3. **Anchor breakfast and lunch to home.** Coffee + breakfast sandwich + lunch out 5x/week = ~$80–120/week. Meal-prep lunch + home coffee can drop that to $20–30. **$200–400/month recoverable.**
4. **One restaurant meal per week**, intentional and enjoyable, instead of 3–4 mediocre ones. Same enjoyment for half the cost.
5. **Grocery list discipline.** Going in without a list adds an estimated 15–25% to the bill from impulse items.

## Tactics that move smaller amounts

- **Generic store brands** for staples (flour, sugar, pasta, basic spices) - typically 20–40% cheaper, often the same supplier.
- **Frozen vegetables** for cooking - cheaper, less waste, similar nutrition.
- **Cook one big batch on Sunday**, eat from it for 2–3 lunches.
- **Drink water at restaurants.** Drinks (esp. alcohol) are the highest-margin items; skipping them often saves $8–15 per meal.

## What rarely works

- Extreme couponing - high time investment for modest savings unless you're already running a stockpile system.
- Aggressive "no eating out for a month" challenges - strong start, fast collapse. Sustainable moderation beats temporary restriction.
- Switching from one delivery app to another for "promo codes" - net effect is roughly zero, and the convenience habit gets reinforced.

## How to know if you've cut too far

If food shopping or cooking starts feeling like deprivation, you've gone past the optimal point. The goal is *redirected* spending (away from convenience, toward intentional meals), not minimization.
