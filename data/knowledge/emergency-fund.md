# Building an Emergency Fund

An emergency fund is liquid cash set aside to absorb financial shocks without taking on debt. It is the foundation every other financial goal sits on.

## How much to save

The conventional target is **3 to 6 months of essential expenses** — not income. Calculate by summing monthly Needs (rent/mortgage, utilities, groceries, insurance, minimum debt payments, transportation). Multiply by:

- **3 months** if you have stable W-2 income, dual-income household, no dependents.
- **6 months** if you have a single income, dependents, are self-employed, or work in a volatile industry.
- **9–12 months** if you are nearing retirement or have specialized skills with long job-search cycles.

## Where to keep it

The fund must be **liquid and safe**, not invested in equities. Best vehicles:

- **High-yield savings account (HYSA)** — currently yielding 4–5% APY at online banks. Best primary location.
- **Money market account** — similar yields, sometimes with check-writing.
- **Short-term Treasury bills or T-bill ETFs** — for the *upper portion* of a larger fund (state-tax exempt).

Avoid: stocks, long-term bonds, crypto, retirement accounts (penalties on withdrawal).

## How to build it

If you are starting from zero:

1. **Starter fund: $1,000** — get to this fast. It absorbs most common emergencies (car repair, deductibles).
2. **One month of expenses** — pause aggressive debt payoff except minimums until you hit this.
3. **Full 3–6 months** — build alongside debt payoff and retirement contributions.

Automate transfers on payday so the money never reaches your checking account. Treat the fund as off-limits — replenish quickly if used.

## Common mistakes

- Mixing the emergency fund with spending money in the same checking account.
- "Investing" it for higher returns and being forced to sell at a loss during a real emergency.
- Skipping it to chase debt payoff or investing — a single uninsured shock can wipe out years of progress.
