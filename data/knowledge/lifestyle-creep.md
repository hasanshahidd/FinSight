# Lifestyle Creep — How Raises Disappear

**Lifestyle creep** (or "lifestyle inflation") is the quiet phenomenon where your spending rises in lockstep with your income, leaving your savings rate and net-worth growth stagnant despite earning more. It is the single most common reason high earners feel "broke."

## How it happens

Each individual upgrade is small and seemingly justified:

- "I work hard, I deserve a nicer apartment."
- "It's just $50/month more on my car payment."
- "Premium streaming + premium grocery delivery — $80/month total."
- "Eating out twice instead of once a week."

None of these is a bad decision in isolation. The compounding is the problem: a 5% raise becomes a 5% spending increase, year after year.

## How to detect it in your own data

The simplest signal: **savings rate over time**. Compute monthly:

> savings rate = (income − spending) / income

Plot it. If your income trended up but your savings rate stayed flat or fell, you have lifestyle creep. The dollar-amount of savings may be growing — but as a *proportion*, you're saving the same fraction of a bigger income, which means lifestyle absorbed every dollar of the raise.

A second signal: **fixed costs as % of income**. Rent, car payments, insurance, subscriptions. If these were 45% three years ago and 55% now (despite a 30% raise), creep has crept.

## The "save half of every raise" rule

The simplest defense: when income rises, route 50% of the increase straight to savings/investments before it reaches your checking account. The other 50% can fund lifestyle — but the savings rate ratchets up automatically.

Variations: some advocate 100% of the raise → savings until you hit a target rate (e.g., 25%), then revert to 50%.

## Common high-leverage creep targets to watch

In rough order of how much they tend to inflate:

1. **Housing.** Moving to a "nicer" place is the single biggest accelerator of creep.
2. **Cars.** New car, leasing, longer loan terms — often $300–500/month more than necessary.
3. **Subscription tier upgrades.** Streaming bundles, software premium plans, gym tiers.
4. **Eating out frequency** (not price-per-meal — frequency).
5. **Travel.** Once-a-year vacations becoming 3–4x/year.
6. **Personal services.** House cleaning, lawn care, dog walking — convenient but recurring.

## What's *not* lifestyle creep

Investments aren't creep. Paying off debt isn't creep. Building an emergency fund isn't creep. The test is: does this expense compound in the future (savings, investing, debt elimination), or is it consumed now? Compounding = good direction; consumed = potential creep.

## When creep is the right choice

You don't need to live like a college student forever. Some creep is healthy:

- After hitting savings goals (emergency fund full, 401(k) maxed, on track for retirement)
- For one-time experiences with high memory return (a meaningful trip)
- For genuine quality-of-life upgrades you've thought about (not impulse)

The goal is *intentional* creep, not invisible drift.
